Cationic and amphipathic peptides for efficient gene delivery:

One of the primary focus areas of the laboratory is to understand the basic tenets of efficient non-viral nucleic acid delivery. In this context, we have developed series of cationic and amphipathic peptides which can form nanocomplexes with different types of nucleic acids and deliver them efficiently to a wide range of cell types with minimal toxicity. The choice of amino acids, total charge, primary and secondary amphipathicity, total hydrophobicity and role of spacers were specific parameters that were looked into in this process. This was coupled with understanding of different barriers involved in the process of gene delivery- starting from understanding the mechanism of cellular entry, intracellular routing and retention, as well as the balance of condensation and release between the carrier and cargo. Currently, these systems are being adapted for delivery of not only plasmid DNA and siRNA but also RNP complexes in CRISPR-Cas systems.

Topical delivery to the skin using a peptide carrier:

Topical delivery of nucleic acids to skin has huge prospects towards therapeutic interventions for cutaneous disorders. The skin, however, poses a challenge for delivery of macromolecules because of its structure. The stratum corneum on top followed by the viable epidermis below â€“ do not allow easy passage of hydrophilic molecules like nucleic acids to efficiently enter the uncompromised skin. Our lab has developed an amphipathic peptide Mgpe9 that can penetrate the uncompromised skin utilizing multiple routes, enter skin cells and deliver plasmid DNA efficiently in vitro and in vivo. We observe efficient gene expression up to the basal layer of the skin without any observable toxic effects after delivery of reporter plasmids. The system can be effectively coupled with formulation bases like silicone oil-thereby improving the applicability. Currently we are working on application of this peptide for delivery of anti-freeze DNA to the skin towards prevention of cold-induced injuries. We have also attempted to use this method for delivery of other macromolecules to the skin.We have successfully complexed chondroitin sulfate, a type of glycosaminoglycan with important role in skin physiology, with an amphipathic peptide and showed its stability at physiological and acidic pH . The nanocomplexes were shown to enter skin cells and also demonstrated anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties in presence of UV irradiation. The nanocomplexes containing chondroitin sulphate could not only enter artificial skin membrane (mimicking the human skin), and mouse skin tissue unlike free chondroitin sulfate but also offer protection from UV-induced damage in mouse skin in vivo. Thus, a completely noninvasive method was devised to deliver a macromolecule into the skin without using injections or abrasive procedures.

Glycosaminoglycans: old players in new roles

Research in our laboratory has also helped us decipher novel roles of cell surface and exogenous glycosaminoglycans in cellular entry and intracellular trafficking of nanocomplexes. Glycosaminoglycans (GAGs) expressed ubiquitously on the cell surface are known to interact with a variety of ligands to mediate different cellular processes. We have shown that peptide chemistry is one of the key factors that determine the dependence on cell surface glycosaminoglycans for cellular internalization and gene delivery. On the other hand, exogenously added GAGs like chondroitin sulphate can be used to enhance the transfection efficiency of peptide-DNA nanocomplexes. A plethora of factors like enhanced stability of the nanocomplexes through formation of a coating, reduction of surface positive charge, different intracellular routing mechanism as well as altered endosomal escape have been found to be responsible for this. In addition, the nature of the peptide in the nanocomplex also plays a role. In light of this understanding, we are also looking into appropriate chemical modifications in chondroitin sulphate to enable penetration of mucus barrier and blood-brain barrier. We anticipate nanocomplexes coated with chemically modified CS to have important therapeutic implications in lung and brain gene delivery systems.

Multimodal action of inorganic and organic nanoparticles

Zinc oxide (ZnO) nanostructures exhibit diverse chemical, biological, and clinical applications. Their unique properties like high refractive index and UV absorption over a wide wavelength range have been utilized for many applications in the field of biomedical science. One of the most common applications of ZnO is in the area of sunscreen agents. While microparticles are usually applied-their opaque appearance and restricted localization including possibility of washing off can be considered as potential drawbacks. ZnO nanoparticles can offer an alternative in this context. However, hydrolysis of ZnO nanoparticles in the skin folds leads to the formation of labile Zn2+ ions, which permeate into viable epidermis and may elicit toxicity. Another major concern is the aggregation propensity of ZnO nanoparticles primarily in biological milieu. We have developed an amphipathic peptide-coated ZnO nanoparticle assembly for improved stabilization of the nanoparticles and studied their UV-protective activity. We observed that the peptide-modified nanoparticles showed lower aggregation, significant reduction in Zn2+ leaching in vitro and even inside the cells for smaller particles, reduced photocatalytic activity, and reduced cellular phototoxicity under UV-B-treated conditions.

We have also developed an antibacterial application using ZnO nanoparticles. Our strategy here was to develop a dispersion of ZnO nanoparticles in ionic liquids which showed superior antibacterial efficacy in comparison to that shown by the individual components. We also delved into the mechanism of killing of a skin-specific bacterium using this system and showed that this is possibly through the production of reactive oxygen species leading to bacterial cell lysis. Further, we showed that this formulation is biocompatible and nontoxic to normal keratinocyte cells even under coculture conditions.

Currently we are also utilizing the leaching of Zn2+ release from zinc oxide nanoparticles to selectively kill melanoma cells. A nanoassembly with a conjugated apoptotic peptide which can release in the intracellular conditions in cancer cells, coupled with the ROS generation through Zn2+leaching is being used to induce selective cell killing in melanoma model.

Our laboratory is also delving into developing drug delivery solutions for Triple Negative Breast Cancer. Triple Negative Breast Cancer (TNBC) refers to any subgroup of breast tumors that do not express the genes for estrogen receptor (ER), progesterone receptor (PR) or human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2) and are therefore hard to target. Some novel therapeutics are like PARP, mTOR inhibitors, TRAIL receptor agonists, etc that try to bring about cancer cell death synergistically along with chemotherapeutic drugs are being developed. In all of the above cases, targeting is of utmost importance, in absence of which the treatment will harm the non-cancerous cells as well. We are currently developing multiple systems based on organic nanoparticle carrier that will deliver drugs selectively to cancer cells.
